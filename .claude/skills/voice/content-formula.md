# Content Formula: The User's Formula

Six-beat scaffold for post-length content: LinkedIn essays, blog articles, talk arcs, long-form thought-leadership pieces, podcast scripts. Skip for short Slack messages, replies, status updates.

## The structure

```
[Hook / Challenge]
    + [Personal Story / Experience]
    + [Deeper Question]
    + [Practical Insight]
    + [Human Impact]
    + [Call to Adventure]
```

## Beat-by-beat

### Hook / Challenge

Open with the friction, not the solution.

- Concrete pain the reader feels.
- Could be a small observation (preferred), a specific moment, or a quick framing of something common-but-unnamed.
- Avoid stat-shaped boasts and mythic framing. When research backs the experience, it belongs mid-post as validation, not in the hook. See **Pattern: Research-backed posts** below.

> *"Sprint 1 with AI agents looks worse than the sprint before. Most teams quit there."*

**Failure mode if you skip:** opening with the solution loses readers who haven't felt the problem. The hook earns the rest.

### Personal Story / Experience

Anchor the hook in lived experience. Named people, real moments, specific numbers.

> *"Sprint 1-2 are exhausting. Sprint 3-4 you break even. Sprint 5-6 you're faster. I've worked through that curve with a dozen teams now."*

The story is the credential. Skip "in our 20 years..." preamble. Let the story carry the experience.

**Failure mode if you skip:** without lived experience, the post reads as theory. Theory without skin doesn't travel.

### Deeper Question

The reframe. What if the surface problem isn't the real problem?

> *"What if the dip isn't failure? What if it's the cost of the foundation?"*

This is where conviction enters, and where peer-language replaces vendor-language.

**Failure mode if you skip:** the post stays at the symptom level. Readers nod, then forget. The reframe is what makes them remember.

### Practical Insight

The actual answer. What works.

> *"In the first two weeks, don't measure throughput. Measure how much the team has encoded into CLAUDE.md and shared agents."*

Concrete. Specific. Often tied to one of the recurring h&w content themes.

**Failure mode if you skip:** a reframe without a practical move reads as smart commentary. Readers want a thing they can do Monday.

### Human Impact

Why this matters beyond the technical fix. The translate-up to engineering culture, team health, programming joy.

> *"By sprint 6, the conversation in standup changes. Less 'why is this broken'. More 'what should we build next'."*

This beat separates h&w content from vendor content. Vendors stop at the technical fix; h&w connects it to people.

**Failure mode if you skip:** the post lands as a technical recipe. Recipes don't move CTOs. Connecting the technical fix to team health is what does.

### Call to Adventure

Forward motion, not summary. A specific next step framed as possibility.

> *"Where's your team going to be in six sprints?"*

Match the channel: a real question for LinkedIn, a clear next step for blog/long-form, a Monday-morning first move for a talk.

**Failure mode if you skip:** ending on the human-impact beat leaves the reader nodding, not moving. The Call closes the energy loop.

## The user's formula quickcard

```
Hook:      The friction the reader feels right now
Story:     Lived experience, named, specific
Question:  What if the surface problem isn't the real one?
Insight:   The Monday-morning move
Impact:    Why this matters beyond the technical fix
Adventure: Forward motion, not summary
```

## Pattern: Research-backed posts

When citing external research (whitepaper, study, survey), the instinct is to lead with the source name. Resist it.

**Structure:**
1. **Open with h&w's lived experience.** State what we observe in practice, before any data. "We've seen it enough times that we can predict it before the team can."
2. **Bring in the data as validation mid-post.** "That turns out to be a measurable pattern." The data confirms what we already know, not the other way around.
3. **Cite figures naturally in the body.** No source name inline, just the numbers.
4. **Single attribution at the end.** One `(Source: Name, date)` line after the closing question, before hashtags.

**Why it matters:** Opening with a study name shifts authority to the study. Opening with lived experience positions h&w as the practitioner who recognizes a pattern, for which research happens to exist. The difference is the difference between "a study found" and "here's what we see, and the numbers back it up."

**Anti-pattern:** "[Source] just put a number on why." Leads with the source name and hands the credibility to them.
